# Chapter 19: AI Makes Prototyping Cheap, Not Thinking Cheap

## Product question

> What is the smallest prototype that can answer the riskiest product question without pretending to be a production capability?

## Five-minute walkthrough

1. Write one learning question and the decision it will change.
2. Identify the target user, scenario, and riskiest assumption.
3. Label real, simulated, hard-coded, omitted, and production-like behavior.
4. Choose fidelity from the decision—not visual polish.
5. Define evidence, limits, stop rule, and what would make the prototype dangerous to reuse.

## Take it to your product

Create one Prototype Contract before prompting an agent to build. Test observed behavior separately from stated opinion, and let the result revise the brief rather than merely adding features.

## Ask an AI coding agent

> Read this guide and the Prototype Contract. Ask for the product question and riskiest assumption before discussing screens or code. Help me choose the smallest artifact that can teach us something. Require labels for real, simulated, hard-coded, missing, and production behavior, plus an evidence plan and stop rule. Challenge scope that does not improve the decision.

## Evidence boundary

A prototype is a learning instrument, not evidence of architecture, reliability, security, maintainability, scale, or market demand. Cheap generation increases the need to label shortcuts and stop building when the question changes.
